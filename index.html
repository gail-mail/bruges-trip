Minnewater Lake

Lake of Love - Free - Always open

Free entry Always open

A calm lake with white swans, weeping willows, and walking paths. Quiet in the late morning before tour groups arrive. A good way to ease into the day before lunch.

Open in Maps
Historical background

Minnewater was built in the 13th century as a water basin for the canal system that connected Bruges to the North Sea port of Damme. Ships carrying goods into the city used the basin as a waiting area while passage through the city locks was arranged.

The white swans on the lake are required by city law. In 1488, the citizens of Bruges held Maximilian I, Holy Roman Emperor, captive for several months during a revolt against his regency over the Low Countries. After the city was forced to release him, Maximilian required Bruges to maintain swans on its waterways as a permanent reminder of the humiliation done to his crown. The city has kept swans ever since.

The name "Lake of Love" comes from a local legend about a woman named Minna who died waiting for her soldier to return.

Source: Visit Bruges (visitbruges.be)

Belfry of Bruges

Belfort - Tickets purchased (14:00-14:20 entry)

Tickets purchased: 2:00-2:20pm entry 366 steep narrow steps - no elevator

The Belfry is 83 metres tall with 366 steps to the top. The view from the top covers the entire city and the surrounding flat countryside. It is one of the most recognisable buildings in Belgium. Open daily from 9:30am to 5pm, last entry at 4:15pm. Timed entry tickets have been purchased for the 2:00-2:20pm entry window. Tickets are available for download below.

Open in Maps
Historical background

Construction on the Belfry began in the 13th century. The octagonal upper section was added in the 15th century. For several hundred years the tower served as the civic centre of the city. Its treasury chambers held town charters and municipal funds. A watchman lived near the top and rang the bells to signal fires, enemy approach, and the time of day.

The carillon of 47 bells is still played by a trained carillonist today. The tower appears on the Belgian two-euro coin.

In 1999, UNESCO added the Belfry to its World Heritage List as part of the designation for the Belfries of Belgium and France.

Source: UNESCO World Heritage List, entry 943

Burg Square

De Burg - Free outdoor square

Free Outdoor square

One of the most architecturally rich squares in Belgium, with the Gothic City Hall on one side and the Basilica of the Holy Blood on another. Stop, look around, and take it in. If the door to the Basilica is open, step inside for a few minutes. Entry is free and it takes only a few minutes.

Open in Maps
Historical background

The square takes its name from the fortified castle built here around 865 by Baldwin Iron Arm, the first Count of Flanders. The name Burg comes from the Latin word for a fortified settlement. The site has been the political centre of Bruges for over a thousand years.

The Gothic City Hall on the square was built between 1376 and 1420 and is one of the oldest town halls in the Low Countries. Its facade is covered with stone statues of the counts and countesses of Flanders.

The Basilica of the Holy Blood was founded in the 12th century. It holds a cloth said to be stained with the blood of Christ, brought to Bruges from the Holy Land by Thierry of Alsace, Count of Flanders, around 1150. The relic has been kept in the city for nearly nine centuries and is still carried through the streets each year in the Procession of the Holy Blood, a Catholic religious event held since the 13th century.

Source: Visit Bruges (visitbruges.be)

Chez Albert - Waffles

Breidelstraat - No reservation needed

Order the Liege waffle, not the Brussels waffle

Sit down, rest our feet, eat a waffle. The Liege waffle is the one to order: dense, chewy, made from a brioche-style dough with pearl sugar that caramelises on the outside during cooking. It needs no toppings.

Open in Maps
Liege vs. Brussels waffles

The Liege waffle was created in the 18th century in the kitchens of the Prince-Bishop of Liege. The dough is enriched with eggs and butter, closer to a brioche than a batter, and pearl sugar is mixed in before cooking. The sugar melts and caramelises against the waffle iron, forming a sticky, golden crust. The waffle is round and irregular in shape. It is a street food and is eaten plain, at room temperature.

The Brussels waffle is different in every way: it is rectangular, much lighter, made from a batter leavened with beaten egg whites, and is typically served with whipped cream, strawberries, or chocolate. It is a dessert or cafe item, not street food.

Belgian waffles became widely known in the United States when Maurice Vermersch sold Liege waffles (rebranded as "Brussels waffles" for American audiences) at the 1964 World's Fair in New York.

Source: Belgian Tourism (belgiumtourism.be)

Groeningemuseum

Dijver 12

Walk-in, no booking needed Closes 5:00pm - leave by 4:45pm for the station

One of the most important art museums in Belgium. The collection covers six centuries of Flemish and Belgian painting. The van Eyck rooms are the highlight. Must-see: "Madonna with Canon van der Paele" (van Eyck, 1436). Open Tuesday to Sunday, 9:30am to 5pm. Walk-in entry, no advance booking needed.

Open in Maps
Historical background

The museum opened in 1930 on the site of the former Eekhout Abbey. Its collection is best known for its Flemish Primitive paintings, a group of works produced in Bruges and other Flemish cities in the 15th and early 16th centuries.

Jan van Eyck lived in Bruges from 1425 until his death in 1441. He worked as court painter to Philip the Good, Duke of Burgundy, who held his court in Bruges. Van Eyck's "Madonna with Canon van der Paele" (1436) was commissioned by Joris van der Paele, a canon of the Cathedral of Saint Donatian in Bruges. The painting shows the donor kneeling before the Virgin and Child. It is widely studied as one of the outstanding examples of early Flemish oil painting.

The collection also holds works by Hans Memling, Gerard David, Hugo van der Goes, Rogier van der Weyden, and later artists including James Ensor and Rene Magritte.

Source: Musea Brugge (museabrugge.be)
